Sonny Angel vs Smiski: Which Series Is Right for You?

Two little icons, two very different vibes. Here's how to tell which blind-box series is really you before you commit your shelf space.

They're the two names every new collector runs into first, and for good reason: Sonny Angel and Smiski are the friendly front door to the whole blind-box hobby. Both are small, both are addictive, and both come from Japanese maker Dreams Inc. But they scratch very different itches. One is a cherubic little sunbeam; the other is a shy green creature who'd rather be tucked behind your books. Here's how to tell which one is really you before you commit your shelf space (and your wallet).

The vibe: sunshine baby vs. bashful gremlin

Sonny Angel is a baby-faced figure, usually posed standing, defined by the headgear he wears — fruit, animals, flowers, sweets, the occasional wildly specific themed hat. The whole personality lives up top, which makes the series feel bright, varied, and endlessly re-themeable. Smiski, by contrast, is a soft green glow-in-the-dark creature caught mid-pose: hiding its eyes, leaning on something, peeking around a corner. Where Sonny Angel is about what he's wearing, Smiski is about what he's doing. If you love color and cheer, lean Sonny. If you love a quiet, slightly mischievous character with an inner life, lean Smiski.

Size, display, and where they actually live

Both are pocket-sized and happiest in groups, but they colonize different parts of a room. Sonny Angels line up beautifully on a shelf edge or windowsill — a row of them reads like a little parade. Smiskis are designed to interact with their surroundings: hanging off a monitor, clinging to a shelf lip, hiding among your plants. That makes Smiski a natural fit for a desk and workspace setup, especially since they charge up under a lamp and glow softly once you switch the light off. It's worth planning your display before the figures start multiplying — because they will.

The blind-box experience and rarity

Both series are sold blind, meaning you don't know which figure is inside until you open it. Each series is organized into themed sets, and most sets include a hidden or "secret" figure that shows up far less often than the standard lineup. A few honest things to keep in mind:

  • Duplicates are part of the deal. Buy blind long enough and you'll get doubles — most collectors treat spares as trade fuel.
  • Secrets drive the resale market. Hidden figures and retired themes are what climb in price later, not the common ones.
  • Sealed vs. open matters. If you care about trading or resale, keep boxes and inner bags; if you just want cute on a shelf, open away.
  • Themes retire. Series rotate, so a set you love won't be around forever — which is exactly why collectors move quickly.

Neither series is objectively "rarer" than the other; both use the same basic secret-figure mechanic. Your odds depend on the specific set, not the brand.

Which should you start with?

If you're brand new and want the gentlest on-ramp, Sonny Angel is arguably the easier obsession to begin: the themes are approachable, the figures are forgiving to display, and there's a huge, welcoming community. Smiski rewards collectors who want a little more character and a little more play. Honestly, most people who stick with the hobby end up owning both. The quick verdict

Choose Sonny Angel for bright, varied, shelf-ready cheer and the biggest beginner community. Choose Smiski for a shy, glow-in-the-dark character with personality to spare and a talent for decorating your whole desk.
